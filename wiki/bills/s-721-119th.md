## What This Bill Does

The Border Security Enhancement Act directs $22 billion in new spending toward security infrastructure and personnel at the U.S.–Mexico border. The legislation is divided into three major areas:

- **Physical barriers** ($9 billion): Extends and reinforces existing fencing and wall construction in high-traffic crossing sectors, including the Rio Grande Valley and Tucson sectors.
- **Surveillance technology** ($7 billion): Deploys new ground sensors, surveillance towers, and unmanned aerial systems along the border, and upgrades the existing camera network.
- **Personnel** ($6 billion): Funds the hiring and training of 8,500 new Border Patrol agents and 1,200 new immigration judges to reduce case backlogs in immigration courts.

## Who Does It Affect?

The bill directly affects Border Patrol operations, immigration court proceedings, and communities along the southern border. Supporters argue it will reduce illegal crossings and drug trafficking. Critics — including immigration advocacy groups and several Democratic senators — argue the bill does not address the root causes of migration from Central America and that detention expansion will worsen humanitarian conditions.

## Current Status

The bill passed the Senate in July 2025 on a largely party-line vote of 54–45. It was passed by the House in a modified form in June 2025 and sent to a conference committee to resolve differences between the two versions. The conference report has not yet been voted on.
