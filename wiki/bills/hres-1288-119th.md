The designation would recognize and honor individuals who have experienced financial fraud, identity theft, or other scams. The bill does not create any new federal programs, funding, or regulatory requirements—it is a ceremonial measure that establishes an official day for public awareness and recognition.  The bill would affect the general public by creating an opportunity to acknowledge scam survivors and raise awareness about fraud prevention and victim support. It may encourage organizations, businesses, and government agencies to recognize the day through events, educational initiatives, or public statements. This type of designation is typically used to highlight social issues and direct attention to affected communities.  Key provisions would include the official designation of the specific date (May 14, 2026) and potentially language encouraging public institutions and private entities to observe the day. Such designations often serve as platforms for advocacy organizations and consumer protection agencies to educate the public about common scams and available resources for survivors."
