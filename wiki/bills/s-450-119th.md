## What This Bill Does

The Universal Healthcare Coverage Act would make every U.S. resident eligible for Medicare, the federal health insurance program currently available to Americans age 65 and older and people with certain disabilities.

Key provisions include:

- **Universal Medicare eligibility**: All U.S. residents — regardless of age, employment status, or immigration status — would be eligible to enroll in Medicare.
- **Out-of-pocket cap**: Individual annual out-of-pocket costs (premiums, deductibles, and copays combined) would be capped at $2,000 for individuals and $4,000 for families.
- **Comprehensive coverage**: The bill expands Medicare's covered services to include dental, vision, hearing, and mental health care at parity with physical health coverage.
- **Prescription drug negotiation**: The federal government would be authorized to directly negotiate drug prices for all Medicare-covered medications.

## Who Does It Affect?

The bill would most immediately benefit the approximately 28 million Americans who are currently uninsured, as well as those who are underinsured or face high out-of-pocket medical costs. Small business owners who currently struggle to offer employee health insurance would also benefit from an alternative to private employer-sponsored plans.

## How It Is Funded

The bill proposes funding through a combination of a 2.5% payroll tax on wages above $250,000, a 5% tax on investment income for households earning above $400,000, and savings from federal drug price negotiations. Supporters argue the elimination of private insurer overhead and administrative costs would offset much of the new spending; critics contend the cost estimates are too optimistic.

## Current Status

The bill passed the Senate with 51 votes in April 2025. It has not yet received a vote in the House of Representatives, where it faces significant opposition. Speaker leadership has not scheduled it for a floor vote.
